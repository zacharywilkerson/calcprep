Question of the Day (Calculus 2)

Question: #1

Tags: Derivatives, Quotient Rule

Date: 05/16/2022

Today, we are taking a look at a problem that involves finding the slope of a tangent line:

What is the slope of the line tangent to the graph of \(y=\frac{x^2-2}{x^2+3}\) when \(x=1\)?

To begin solving this problem, we should recognize that the slope of the line tangent to the graph is equivalent to finding the slope. Therefore, this question wants us to take the derivative of the given function and then evaluate the derivative at \(x=1\). We should also recognize that, in order to take this derivative, we will need to use the quotient rule.

Let's go ahead and apply the quotient rule and see what we get:

$$ \begin{eqnarray} y &=& \frac{x^2-2}{x^2+3} \\ y' &=& \frac{(x^2+3)(2x)-(x^2-2)(2x)}{(x^2+3)^2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $$

Note that we chose not to simplify the derivative equation above, although we could have. Plugging in for \(x\) right away, as opposed to simplifying the equation and then plugging in, is the quicker approach.

Now, we need to evaluate this derivative at \(x=1\) (the point given):

$$ \begin{eqnarray} y'(1) &=& \frac{((1)^2+3)(2(1))-((1)^2-2)(2(1))}{((1)^2+3)^2} \\ &=& \frac{(4)(2)-(-1)(2)}{16} = \frac{10}{16} = \frac{5}{8} \end{eqnarray} $$

And there we have it. By applying the quotient rule and evaluating the derivative at \(x=1\), we found the slope of the line tangent to the graph of \(y=\frac{x^2-2}{x^2+3}\) when \(x=1\) to be \(\frac{5}{8}\).

Key Takeaways

Slope of tangent line = derivative

Use quotient rule when taking the derivative of rational functions (functions that are fractions and have polynomials in the numerator and denominator)
